Foundations

Set Theory and Metric Spaces, Irving Kaplansky

Kaplansky's book is old-school, at times archaic, and uses fairly out-moded notation and terminology. It's also, in my opinion, an excellent treatise on the elements of axiomatic set theory and metric spaces. Beginning with sets, set operations, and cardinality and continuing to cardinal and ordinal arithmetic and ZFC axioms. In the next section, it covers metric spaces, then completeness and compactness, and finally product spaces, the fixed-point theorem, and a teeny bit of category theory. It caps off with applications to topology and algebra. And all of that in just under 160 pages! Worth the read, and the problems are insightful without being oppressive.

Algebra

Algebra, Michael Artin

Artin takes a largely computational approach to algebra and teaches linear algebra as a primer (and likely a review) before starting with the standard groups/fields/rings presentation of the subject- which spans the field up to Galois theory.

Analysis

Complex Analysis, Lars Ahlfors

This is generally regarded as one of those textbooks that so-called "serious" mathematicians read in their undergraduate studies. It hits all the major topics: complex numbers and functions, mappings, contour integration, conformal mappings and so on, as well as some of the hot math topics like Riemann Zeta and analytic continuation. Ahlfors' text starts from a peculiar point- assuming very little in the way of knowledge of complex numbers, but simultaneously assuming that the reader has at least basic knowledge of abstract algebra.

I liked this book quite a bit, and found it to be a worthy supplement to the course notes for PMATH 352 at Waterloo. The problems I found to be of adequate difficulty to reinforce learning, without being so hard that they send you immediately to the solutions guide. My biggest criticism of the text is that the geometric allusions are fairly weak, in my opinion. If that were a greater concern, I would have included more figures and made more emphasis of the connections. They seem more like an afterthought.
