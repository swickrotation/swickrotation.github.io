Classical Mechanics

Classical Dynamics of Particles and Systems, J. Marion & S. Thornton

This textbook is really junk. Unfortunately, it's found itself a little niche in the physics curriculum because it's one of the few (and easily the most popular) book written for the level of study you'll probably be at by the time you take your first "real" (non-introductory level) courses on classical mechanics. At Waterloo, the AMATH department uses Taylor's Classical Mechanics, which I hear (but have not confirmed) is a superior text.

Don't get me wrong, M&T has everything you need per se, but it gets lost in itself with clumsy formalism. It attempts to be a more mathematical view of classical mechanics without the elegance it could avail itself of in so-doing. If at all possible, steer clear.

A Course of Theoretical Physics Vol. I: Mechanics, L. Landau & E. Lifshitz

This is arguably one of the most beautiful physics books I've ever read, but it is absolutely not for the faint of heart. Landau takes no prisoners when it comes to exposition: by page three, he's already derived the Euler-Lagrange equation. Do not use this as a first text- instead, save it for when you understand the field better. You'll see just how brilliant it is then.

Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics, V. I. Arnold

This one, with Goldstein, have the reputations as the strongest treatment of classical mechanics at the graduate level. Arnold has written this text with the mathematician in mind, so be prepared for some differential geometry- I'm talking tangent spaces, vector bundles, manifolds, symplectic spaces, the works. However, this will get you prepared to talk general relativity, and apparently gauge theories, so take it in the teeth. You'll be happy you did.

Electrodynamics

Introduction to Electrodynamics, D. J. Griffiths

Without a doubt, this is the strongest of Griffith's three texts. It's a decent, intermediate text that's fairly self-contained. Like all of his books, he's a little wordy, but in a field that's as mathematically nit-picky as electrodynamics, you'll appreciate the exposition for this first text. It's also pretty well the universal undergraduate textbook in this field, and you'll use it for all three courses if you're at UW. I actually recommend buying this one, you'll probably keep it after you graduate.

Classical Electrodynamics, D. Jackson

This book deserves its reputation. It's the grand-daddy of all modern texts on electrodynamics and pulls absolutely no punches when it comes to detail. It is a cornerstone of graduate education in electrodynamics and has been for decades. It is probable, that out of any texts, this is the one your professors have all pored over - some with more fond memories than others. Jackson is careful to do his mathematics properly, which I discovered in particular by seeing the changes between editions. Hard problems, many hours of work await. But you'll know E&M if you can hack it.

Quantum Mechanics

Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, D. J. Griffiths

I personally don't like the approach taken in this text. It's a little clunky, just laying the Schrodinger equation on you on the first page. The philosophy of the book is to "shut up and calculate", which is fine for someone who wants to do well on their finals but doesn't care about how the theory is developed. I don't mean to knock the book, it's just not what I'd pick up if I had a choice. The treatment of the hydrogen atom in chapter 4 was pretty solid, as long as you remember special functions pretty well. I just wouldn't trust the text as a referrence for long-term use. I traded my copy to a friend for his copy of Wald's General Relativity, and haven't looked back.

Modern Quantum Mechanics, J. J. Sakurai, J. Napolitano

This is the standard amongst first year graduate students taking courses in quantum mechanics. In truth, I haven't read this book all-the-way through, but what I've read so far is solid. The main author, Sakurai, died before finishing the book, and apparently this caused the last two chapters to suffer. The only complaint that I can bring to bare on the text thus far is that Sakurai skips many steps, and that makes following his arguments rather painful at times. I can understand trying to cut down to make your arguments elegant, but at times I've had to take up a board to understand some things that Sakurai simply writes in a line. Anyway, the "modern" part is really apt- Sakurai organizes the entire text around symmetry principles and infinitessimal generators. Also, Sakurai himself only wrote the first 10 chapters- he died before finishing the second edition of the text, and Jim Napolitano finished the book. The quality drops off there- but by the time you get to the end of the book, there are better texts covering that material anyway.

Thermodynamics and Statistical Mechanics

Introduction to Thermal Physics, Daniel V. Schroeder

I have not come across an introductiory thermodynamics textbook that has left me satisfied, but Schroeder comes the closest. It's fairly comprehensive in material with plenty of applications, and when it doesn't cover a topic, the author generally points out resources for supplementary study. To its credit, it has plenty of problems with a wide range of approaches- some practical and involving physical participation of the reader with some equipment, and some more theoretically grounded questions illustrating links between thermodynamics and other fields of physics. Where I lose patience with it is the fairly sloppy notation used throughout the text- in particular with the fast and loose use of infinitessimals, and how Schroeder seems to pick and choose when to call things infinitessimal and when not to. It gets easier to manage as the reader progresses, but the result is extra work on the reader's part without actually gaining extra understanding. I think the first 4 chapters are about sufficient for a first course in thermal physics- and when they're finished, I think the reader may as well move on to a textbook in straight-up statistical mechanics. But if you're looking for a primer text with plenty of decent practice problems written in a more vernacular style, you're not going to do better with any other modern text.

General Relativity

General Relativity, R. Wald

As far as self-contained texts on the subject go, I think this one's the top. It starts from the very foundations of general relativity, differential geometry. Starting in chapter two with the first notions of abstract coordinate spaces, vectors and one-forms, and coordinate transformations on manifolds. You'll also learn some things about tensor fields. After that it's curvature, derivatives, tangent space, and geodesics. Only after covering these topics do you get to start doing the physics- but once you do, it evolves very naturally. This text isn't superfluous or lost in mathematical detail, but it is precise and concrete enough that any reader with mathematical inclinations will not be lost in how loose physicists usually write. The caveat is, that for a text as long as it is, it's lacking worked examples that one would expect- a disadvantage that is particularly difficult to deal with if the text is indeed your first introduction to the mathematics involved. It is my opinion that this can be mitigated with a solid differential gemoetry text. See Lee's Introduction to Smooth Manifolds

Quantum Information

An Introduction to Quantum Computing, P. Kaye, R. LaFlamme, M. Mosca

This book gets a bit of a bad rap, for some reasons that are pretty fair, but for some other reasons that are not-so-fair. It was designed essentially as a compillation of the lecture notes and supplementary information for PHYS 467 / CS 467 / CO 481, which is a pretty difficult course to teach. With so many students coming from these disperate backgrounds, it's really hard to flesh out each concept in total detail. KLM doesn't really try to do that. Rather, it offers a light introduction with the major points exposed and explained (though in the case of theorems, unproved). In this regard, it's actually fairly well-contained in my opinion, as a supplement to the lecture material, and to other texts and papers. The exercises are decently well-spaced and reflective of what you'd see on assignments and teach you some necessary skills and techniques. The flaw is to think of the text as totally sufficient on its own to understand the material. This is due to both the noted lightness of the exposition, as well as some unfortunate errors in the text itself. It will likely be well-revised for the next edition.

Quantum Computation and Quantum Information, M. Nielsen, I. Chuang

Nielsen and Chuang's textbook is often referred to in a biblical sense. It's a solid, absolutely comprehensive introductory text on QI/QC with enough background material that it could be handed to a talented sophmore and with enough time an effort, they could most likely start reading research papers without having to do too much outside consulting. I used this text's chapter 7 as a major source when taking PHYS 468 - the discussion of physical quantum computers was gentle enough that even your most abstract theorist won't be too lost. Chapters 8, 11, and 12 were major sources for my final paper for PHYS 467, which was on concentration of partially entangled bits. It's a quality book, with a place on the bookshelf of any aspiring quantum information theorist.
