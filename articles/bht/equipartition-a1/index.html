Appendix A - Ideal Gas Law from the Equipartition Theorem

May 2019

1. Introduction

While we're usually introduced to the Ideal Gas Law as an empirical result in its own right. The lucky among us may seen a loose derivation of it as a combination of Charles's law, Boyle's law, the Guy-Lussac law, and Avogadro's rule somewhere around highschool chemistry. Ultimately, it is often taught early enough that the student is expected to absorb it and take it for granted.

Interestingly, though, we can actually find a derivation of the ideal gas law from the equipartition theorem and Hamiltonian mechanics. While I will not go into so much detail as to re-establish Hamiltonian mechanics from first principles, it is worth the time to re-produce the generalised equipartition theorem: itself a foundational result of statistical mechanics.

However, as is often the case with foundational results, we do not come by this most important theorem simply. In fact, it is a result which to me is best proven at the end of a long road beginning with a mathematical object called a "symplectic manifold". We will delve into the particulars to varying degrees of depth, as criticality dictates.

2. Geometrical Notions

Before we can talk about symplectic manifolds however, we need to have a slightly better understanding of differential geometry. Much of this portion is going to be summary as we will have covered the fundaments of the theory in the preceding entries. To wit, I am going to assume the reader is familiar with basic concepts in topology. We start with some definitions.

DEFINITION A1.2.1: a n-dimensional topological manifold $M$ is a pointed topological space that fulfills the following three properties:

  1. $M$ is Hausdorf: for $p,q \in M, \exists U, V \subset M$ such that $p \in U, q \in V, U \cap V = \emptyset$. In other words, any two points in the topological space $M$ may be expressed as members of disjoint neighbourhoods.
  2. $M$ is Second-Countable: $\exists \{A_i\}$ such that $M \subset \bigcup_i A_i, \left|\{A_i\}\right| \leq \aleph_0$. Again in plain language, this means that there are a countable (if not finite) union of topological spaces that when "glued together" contain the topological space $M$.
  3. $M$ is Locally n-Euclidean: each point $x \in M$ has a neighbourhood homeomorphic to an open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$, i.e. $\forall x \in M \exists$ open $U \subset M$ such that $x \in U$ and a homeomorphism $\phi$ that takes this neighbourhood into an open set in $\tilde{U} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$: $\phi:U \rightarrow \tilde{U}$. We remind ourselves that a homeomorphism is a continuous bijective map with a continuous inverse.
