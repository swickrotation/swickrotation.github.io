Quantum Angular Momentum

September 2019

Sooner rather than later, the novice student will come across the term "spin" in the context of quantum mechanics. Many of these starting students will have some vague notion that quantum spin is not the same thing as classical rotation. Eventually (and likely sooner rather than later) the student will become disatisfied with simply repeating this fact over and over, and realize that knowledge and understanding are not always the same thing.

What we call "spin" is a measure of intrinsic angular momentum. It's existence can be inferred from the Stern-Gerlach experiment. From this experiment, it is clear that the magnetic moment of atomic-scale objects takes quantized values - owing to the discrete concentrations of particles deflected that are observed, rather than the continuous distribution one would expect in a classical system.

We account for this discrete set of concentrations by introducing a "magnetic dipole moment". A result of classical electrodynamics is that rotating, electrically charged systems give rise to a magnetic dipole. In a classical setting, we may visualize an electron as a charged particle rotating about some axis with angular momentum $\boldsymbol{J}$. Such a particle will have a dipole moment of $$ \boldsymbol{\mu} = \frac{-e}{2m_e}\boldsymbol{J}. $$ Classically speaking, this angular momentum takes no consideration of spin. Taking corrections from quantum mechanics into account, we see that a better value for our magnetic moment is given by $$ \boldsymbol{\mu} = g\frac{-e}{2m_e}\boldsymbol{J}, $$ where $g$ is a unitless constant of proportionality. This, still, is not the full picture: in real physics, the electron is better treated as a point particle and does not in fact carry an angular momentum in the sense we usually think of.

In quantum mechanics, angular momentum is composed of two quantities, rotational angular momentum and spin angular momentum. We write, generally, that $\boldsymbol{J} = \boldsymbol{L} + \boldsymbol{S}$, with classical angular momentum being $\boldsymbol{L}$ and spin angular momentum being $\boldsymbol{S}$. Both of these components are quantized: they take discrete steps up and down in terms of magnitude.
