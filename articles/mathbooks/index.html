Foundations

Set Theory and Metric Spaces, Irving Kaplansky

Kaplansky's book is old-school, at times archaic, and uses fairly out-moded notation and terminology. It's also, in my opinion, an excellent treatise on the foundational elements of set theory and metric spaces. The first half of this concise volume (just under 160 pages!) is devoted to developing set algebra in both the finite and infinite cases, developing a motivation for discussions of cardinality and then cardinal and ordinal arithmetic. It completes this portion by introducing the ZFC axioms which is nice to have, though properly axiomatic set theory deserves its own treatment in my opinion. In the next section, he covers the basics of metric spaces: completeness, compactness, product spaces, and some more interesting results like Brauer's fixed-point theorem, and a teeny bit of category theory. It caps off with the obvious next steps in point-set topology and connections between set theory and algebra.

As a personal aside, this was the first "serious" math textbook I ever read. It was passed to me by a senior student while I was in freshman back in '13. I enjoyed it very much then, even if it was a little hard to parse - especially without knowing any pure mathematics students at that time. Once one gets the hang not just of Kaplansky's style but of how to read and write mathematical proofs, and what real proof entails, this text is smooth sailing.

Algebra

Algebra, Michael Artin

Artin has an unorthodox approach to algebra that is not to everyone's taste. He starts with linear algebra rather than the classic groups-rings-fields approach, and only later gets to what one normally thinks of as the "abstract algebra". This isn't a bad thing, it's just a thing. I personally found it helpful to start where it did given that my background in linear algebra was already very strong when I read his book, so the primer founding itself in somewhere where my footing was solid made understanding the later sections considerably simpler.

Analysis

Complex Analysis, Lars Ahlfors

This is generally regarded as one of those textbooks that so-called "serious" mathematicians read in their undergraduate studies. It hits all the major topics: complex numbers and functions, mappings, contour integration, conformal mappings and so on, as well as some of the hot math topics like Riemann Zeta and analytic continuation. Ahlfors' text starts from a peculiar point- assuming very little in the way of knowledge of complex numbers, but simultaneously assuming that the reader has at least basic knowledge of abstract algebra. It has some dated terminology as well, which isn't too surprising.

I liked this book quite a bit, and found it to be a worthy supplement to the course notes for PMATH 352 at Waterloo. The problems I found to be of adequate difficulty to reinforce learning, without being so hard that they send you immediately to the solutions guide. My biggest criticism of the text is that the geometric allusions are fairly weak. If that were a greater concern, I would have included more figures and made more emphasis of the connections. They seem more like an afterthought. Perhaps something like "visual complex analysis" would be a good additional reference to those wanting a better geometric intuition.
